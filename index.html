Introduction

Life expectancy, nationally and in West Sussex, has increased in the last 20 years overall (Global Burden of Disease, 2019) but there is a gap of almost four years between males and females. Healthy life expectancy (the number of years an average person might expect to live in good, disease free, life) has also increased but the gain in healthy life expectancy has not been as high compared to gains in overall life expectancy. Similar to overall life expectancy, male healthy life expectancy trails behind females although the gap is much smaller at just over one years difference. Whilst females may live longer lives than males, in West Sussex and nationally, females tend to spend more of their lives living with disability or in 'sub-optimal' health, compared to males.

Health inequalities are unfair and often avoidable differences in health across the population, and between different groups within society based on their sex (such as with life expectancy) but it can also arise from factors such as our age, race, sexual orientation, where we are born, work, and where we live. Indeed, the gap in life expectancy at birth between the most and least deprived areas in England overall is around nine years for males and almost eight years for females (Office for National Statistics, 2021). This means that those living in the most deprived parts of the country could expect to die around eight or nine years earlier than those in the least deprived areas of England. The gap in healthy life expectancy is doubled at around 19 years for males and females living in the least deprived areas compared to their counterparts in the most deprived areas.

The extra years in sub-optimal health place even further pressure on health services as well as having an impact on employment and productivity. The additional costs to the NHS of health inequalities have been estimated at almost £5 billion per year from the greater use of hospital services by people in deprived areas alone (Asaria, Doran, & Cookson, 2016).

The approach to acting on health inequalities was established in the NHS Long Term Plan (NHS England, 2019 (1)), prior to the COVID-19 global pandemic and the rationale and urgency for this work has only increased in response to the burden resulting from the pandemic and as services recover. There has never been a more important moment to coordinate an effort to reduce the gap in health inequalities.

High quality services on their own can reduce unwarranted variation in outcomes. However, they will not reduce inequalities at a population level unless they also identify (with partners) and give graduated and targetted support to populations in greatest need, who may not be using those services to the best effect.

The Core20PLUS5 approach

The ‘Core20PLUS5’ initiative is to drive targeted health inequalities improvements in the following areas:

  • Core 20 – Most deprived 20% of our population
  • PLUS – Other population groups as identified by local population health data such as ethnic minority communities
  • 5 – Targeting five key clinical areas of health inequalities:
    • Early Cancer diagnosis (screening & early referral) 75% of cases diagnosed at stage 1 or 2 by 2028
    • Hypertension case finding allowing for interventions to optimise blood pressure and minimise risk of myocardial infarction and stroke
    • Chronic Respiratory disease (driving Covid & Flu vaccination uptake) a clear focus on Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease driving up uptake of vaccines to reduce infective exacerbations and emergency hospital admissions resulting from these exacerbations
    • Annual health checks for people with Serious Mental Illness 60% of those living with Severe Mental Illness (SMI) receiving annual health checks
    • Continuity of maternity carer plan ensure continuity of care for 75% of women from Black, Asian and minority ethnic communities and from the most deprived groups.

Primary Care Networks

Primary Care Networks (PCNs) are networks of general practices which attempt to integrate primary care with secondary and community services, and bridge a gap between general practice and emerging Integrated Care Systems (Fisher, Thorlby, & Alderwick, 2019). Each PCN is made up a group of a number of GP practices forming a network that covers approximately 30,000 to 50,000 patients.

Directed Enhanced Services (DES) are voluntary add-ons to the core GP contract to incentivise specific services. The specific DES requirements of PCNs are set out in the Network Contract DES Specification (NHS England, 2019 (2)).

The map below shows the geographical footprint of West Sussex PCNs.

Each PCN boundary is made by aggregating Lower-layer Super Output Areas (LSOAs) together. However, it should be noted that GP practices do not have geographical boundaries and more than one GP practice can operate in the same local area. Whilst the map is useful to understand roughly the local areas that each PCN may cover, for planning purposes, in reality these boundaries are not mutually exclusive (e.g. there is some crossover as patients may use GP services outside of their local areas).

We have assigned every LSOA in West Sussex to one PCN based on data from the number of residents in the LSOA registered to each GP practice and some GP practices operate in areas assigned to other PCNs (e.g. Horsham Collaborative practices in Horsham Central areas). PCN boundaries can transcend district and borough boundaries and some even cross into neighbouring counties.

Deprivation in West Sussex

West Sussex overall is relative less deprived, and is in the top 20% of least deprived local authorities in the country. However, a small number of neighbourhoods along the coast in Arun and in Crawley are among the most deprived neighbourhoods in England.

The indices of deprivation

The Indices of Deprivation are an area-based measure of relative levels of deprivation in small areas, (Lower Super Output Areas (LSOAs), which contain approximately 1,500 people). It can be used to compare the deprivation across different areas, identify the most deprived areas within a larger geography (for example in the CCG), and to examine which domains of deprivation are more or less prominent in an area. The Indices cannot be used to quantify how deprived an area is (nor can it say by how much one area is more deprived than another), and there are different indices of deprivation for Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland (so comparisons cannot be made across countries).

The indices are published by the Department for Communities and Local Government every 3-4 years and are widely used, notably in funding allocations and targeting. The most recent update is the ID2019, released in October 2019. ID2019 is made up of seven domains of deprivation, although each domain is not given equal weighting. From these, an overall score of small area deprivation is calculated; these are then ranked from most deprived (1) to least deprived (32,844). It is important to note that the indices measure deprivation and not affluence (the least deprived area may not necessarily be the most affluent).

Domains contributing to the overall Index of multiple deprivation:

  • Income (22.5%)
  • Employment (22.5%)
  • Health Deprivation and Disability (13.5%)
  • Education, Skills and Training (13.5%)
  • Crime (9.3%)
  • Barriers to Housing and Services (9.3%)
  • Living Environment (9.3%)
